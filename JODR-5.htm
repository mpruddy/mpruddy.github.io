V

THE FENIAN MOVEMENT

An Account of its Origin, Progress

and Temporary Collapse.

 

By Jeremiah O'Donovan Rossa

 

The American War and Fenianism—Expected Conflict with England—Suppressing Meetings. Proclamation Read at Union Hall—John Limrick, a Magistrate, Forbidding Irishmen to Land—Another, John Sydney Townsend, Fighting to Defend the Country—Tearing Down Illuminations for the Prince of Wales' Marriage—Demonstration of Sympathy for the Poles—Stopping the Procession—Lowering the Flags—Extinguishing the Torches—Tom Somerville — The Crimean Soldier— Rhyming. James Anthony Fronde — He Tells Truths. Irishmen Must. Fight for Their Freedom Like Men and Strike England to Her Knees.

 

 

The breaking out of War in America made Fenianism active in Ireland. England had taken the side of the South, not for the love of the Southerns, but for the love of seeing the Union disrupted. The English newspapers and English statesmen were proclaiming that the Republic was a failure, and there was no great secret made of the fitting out of ships to aid the Southern cause. The organizers in Ireland, anticipating that England would go so far as to make war between America and England inevitable, redoubled their energy in preparing for the "opportunity." I went to see the American Consul in Queenstown, and had an interview with him; the particulars of that interview it is unnecessary to relate here. England went very far to provoke America at the time, and she might have gone further were it not that Ireland was between England and America and that thousands of Irishmen were ready to strike her on all sides it they only got the chance.

On the other hand the English authorities in Ireland were putting forth all their powers to keep the people in subjection. Though no coercion act was legally in force, the magistrates and police took the law into their own hands and acted as if the Coercion act was a living law. They suppressed meetings and gatherings of the people by threats and by a show of force. The year 1863 in Ireland was a very lively year. The Fenian organization had spread into every province and county, the men were active in getting up public meetings under one pretense or another and alarm was increasing.

In company with five or Six young men I went to Union Hall one Sunday, a village on the seashore, four miles south of Skibbereen; we met some of the friends there, went into a hotel and had some refreshments. Our recruiting company grew large by degrees, and speeches, recitations and songs came to be in order. We left Union Hall about ten o'clock and walked back to Skibbereen. Next morning there was a rumor spread far and wide that the Fenians had landed and wore marching from the sea all night. The women from Union Hall who came into Skibbereen on Monday morning, with their baskets of fresh fish to sell, came into my shop and said they met on the road the magistrate of Union Hall, John Limrick, and that he was raging mad, cursing and swearing that if he caught me in Union Hall again he'd make it a sore day for me. "Tell him," said I, "that I’ll be down there again next Sunday." Those women spoke the Irish language, and it was in the Irish language I spoke to them; but there is no use in my telling my story in the Irish language here; it would be Greek to many of our readers.

The rumor spread through the country that we would go to Union Hall next Sunday again, and that rumor was met by another one from the English side of the house that if we went we would never come back alive; that we would be shot down like dogs. It would never do for us to be intimidated; our cause would lose prestige. Sunday morning came, and after mass and breakfast some twenty or thirty of us from Skibbereen were on the road toward Union Hall. Limrick, the magistrate, had sent out requisitions to all the surrounding police barracks, calling the police to Union Hall that day, and on Sunday morning the police were marching in from Ross, Drinagh, Leap, Drimoleage, Ceharagh, Skibbereen, Glendore and Castletownsend. War and rumors of war were in the air, and the people the country around, seeing the armed police marching on the several roads toward Union Hall, followed them into the little city. The Men from Ross brought a band of music with them. They crossed the bay from Glendore in boats, and as the boats approached the quay at Union Hall Limrick, the magistrate, stood there and forbade them to land. I stood alongside of Limrick and told the men not to be driven back by such petty tyranny as this. That this was Irish soil and they had as good a right to tread it as Limrick had. Patrick and James Donovan, who are now in New York, steered their boat into shallow water and leaped ashore; the other men in the boat leaped after them. The bandsmen went to the house of Father Kingston and remained there for a short time.

Limrick issued orders that all the public houses in the town be closed, and the police went from house to house, ordering the proprietors to shut shop. I was at Mrs. Collins' hotel when Sergeant Curran came in and ordered Mrs. Collins to turn out all who were in the house and lock the doors. I was in the house. I was married to Mrs. Collins' niece. I asked Mrs. Collins would she order me out. She said she would not. There turning to the policeman I said "Now, Sergeant, if you put your hand on me and tell me I must leave the house I'll leave it; otherwise I'll stay here as long as it suits me. "He said he was only obeying orders; that he had no orders to lay hands on me and would not do it. He went out and I remained in. Mrs. Collins then asked me to take the people out, as she'd be sure to lose her license unless she obeyed Limrick's orders and closed the home. So out we went.

As we got on the street the bandsmen were returning from the priests' house with a crowd of the people after them. The police stopped them and ordered them to disperse; Patrick Donovan was arrested by the policemen. Some girls named Dillon, who were first cousins of his, rushed out of their house, took him out of the hands of the police and carried him into the house. Inspector Potter was in command of the police, and he was subject to Linirick's orders. John Sydney Townsend and Dr. Somerville, two other magistrates, were present with Limrick. Limrick ordered Potter to prepare for action and proceeded to read the Riot act. Potter ordered the police to fix bayonets and load. The women in the houses were looking out the windows, and the alarm of their cries so the policemen wore fixing bayonets is still in my mind. I walked up to the three magistrates, saying: "What do you mean by this proceeding. Mr. Limrick "Do you want blood shed hero to-day?" John O'Driscoll, a school teacher.—dismissed a week after for attending that meeting—now in Boston, was by my side, and he asked some other question. Limrick said he wanted no blood shed, but he wanted the disturbance put down. We told him it was he who was creating all the disturbance, and if he wished to carry it farther he might do so and take the consequences. We had no intention to do hurt or harm to any one, but we had a right to tread our native land, and would maintain that right. Patrick Spillane, now in Rochester or Syracuse, then spoke to the magistrate. I looked around, and seeing the band master, Dan O'Donoghue, who, by the way, was a Protestant, struggling with a policeman who was trying to wrest his trombone from him I went over and told Dan to give him the trombone. The magistrates did not give orders to the police to charge or fire, and the day passed off without any bloodshed.

Next day I went into the Beecher Arms Hotel in Skibbereen, and met John Sydney Townsend. He asked me to have a drink. "I will," said I, "after your nearly killing me yesterday." Then he began speaking of the work yesterday. He had been a traveler in his early days, he had been In Australia, and it was from there he got the name of "Sydney." "Now, Mr. Townsend," said I, "If you were in any other land on God's earth but this unfortunate land of ours, and found yourself hunted and banned as we were there yesterday, because you were Irish, wouldn't you resent it, and perhaps resist it?" "Yes, by —; and look at this that I got from a crowd of Englishmen I found running down Ireland"—here he had his coat off and had my hand placed on a dislocated shoulder blade he got in a fight with a crowd of Englishmen in Australia. Occurrences similar to that I described In Union Hall were occurring in other parts of Ireland in connection with the work of organization. It is not a love of egotism but a love of truth that makes me take this one to illustrate the opposition and difficulties that were to be met in the progress of that work ; the things witnessed myself I can speak more correctly of than of things I did not witness.

England's Prince of Wales was married on the 10th of March, 1863, and on that night all who were loyal, or pretended to be loyal to England in Ireland were displaying their loyalty in illuminations. The people who were rebels were not willing that the other brand should be put upon them, and they resisted the imposition of it by smashing the illuminations in many places. In Dublin the students of the Queen's College cut off the gas and left the college in darkness; in rebel Cork the rebels smashed the illuminated windows of the Queen's, old castle, of Sir John Arnott's establishment, and of other illuminated places. In Skibbereen we had a public news room, in which the bylaws, rules and regulations allowed no politics or political discussions or demonstrations. I was a member of that news room. The subscription was a guinea a year. On the evening of the day of the Prince of Wales' marriage John O'Driscoll came into my shop, exclaiming "Rossa, the news room is illuminated! Aren't you a member of it Are you joining in the illuminations?" I walked out and went to the news room, and there, sure enough, were the candles and gewgaws in the window. I went in and I put the Candles out. William Everett, who is now in New York and who was a right hand man of Richard B. Connolly's and a secretary of the St. Patrick's Alliance when I came out here to America from prison, was the Honorary Secretary of our Newsroom Society in Skibbereen; he was the first man to come into the room after I put out the lights. "What is all this about?" said he. "’Tis I ought to ask what is all this about?" said I. "Why was this room illuminated tonight? Don't you know the rules there forbid the introduction of any politics in this room?" "But there was a meeting of members here to-day and they passed a resolution to illuminate the room."Where was I from that meeting, why did not you send me a notice to attend it; have you not been paid my subscription, and am I not a member of this newsroom in good standing?"

"Well, I’Il call another meeting now and have those candles relit."

"You may call the meeting, but I greatly doubt that you'll have the candles relit."

Mr. Everett went out, and Mrs. Doyle, the lady who owned the hotel, came in exclaiming:

"Oh, for goodness sake, Mr. O'Donovan, don't destroy me."

"Never fear, Mrs. Doyle: I won't destroy you; tell me how comes it that this room is illuminated tonight?

"I'll tell you, but for the Lord's sake don't speak of it. Mr. Robinson, the magistrate, gave me £7 to illuminate the whole house, and he thought it would spoil the effect of the illuminations if the newsroom windows were kept dark, so they had a meeting in the newsroom and they passed a resolution to illuminate those windows too."

Here Mr. Everett made his appearance accompanied by several of the members; one by one they came, till about twenty were in the room. Samuel Riordan was voted to the chair, and some one of the Loyalists proceeded to the lighting of the candles in the windows. I looked a little black at that proceeding but said nothing. Doctor Donovan opened the ball; he arraigned me for acting improperly in putting out the lights. I referred to the rules and regulations that forbade politics to enter that room; he said the lighting of the room was no politics; I said it wes, and politics of the basest kind; he said a meeting called that day had decided to light the room; I said I should have been called to that meeting. Father O'Brien said he remarked at the meeting that O'Donovan Rossa as a member would object to the illuminations; Father Davis said he regretted I was not notified to attend the meeting, as it would do away with any justification I may have for protesting, The room was getting hot—hot every way—the candle light, the gaslight and the hot words; the chairman told some one to let down the windows a little. The fight went on, and as I answered every one who assailed me the crowd in the street kept cheering me on; Doctor Donovan said something loyal; the crowd at the window, groaned and hissed at it. "There is no order at this meeting," said he, "there is no law here but mob law." "Well, Mr. Chairman," said I, as the doctor says there is no law here but mob law, and as you allowed him to say it without reprimand, we may as well have the full benefit of that law," and, making for the windows, I tore down the flags, fidols and candles and throw them into the street. I had then to run abide —not from the men in the room, but from the men in the street ; they soon found me out though, tossed me on their shoulders, and I had hardly a stitch of clothes on me when they landed me at home. No, no, the Irish people are not loyal to England; they love not English rule; who say they do say what is most untrue—so notoriously untrue that it may be said they lie.

About this time the Poles were fighting for the freedom of their land. Prince Albert, before he died, said the Poles were as little deserving of sympathy as the Irish, and the Irish thought it would not be amiss to get up some demonstration of sympathy for them. A meeting was announced to take place in Skibbereen and the boys were preparing torchlights for the procession. The Government took alarm, knowing that it was the Irish Revolutionary Brotherhood men were at the back of the demonstration. Not knowing what may be hidden in their designs, not knowing but a rising in rebellion may be contemplated, all the police in the bar[o]nies adjoining wore drawn into the town the night of the demonstration, and the peasantry, seeing the police marching into town, followed them in. The streets were crowded. We got some thousands of handbills printed and got boys to distribute them into the hands of police and people, particularly into the hands of the police. The magistrates were raging mad at our doing such a thing, as the handbill ran this way "To the People—Friends You come together this evening to express your sympathy with a people who, like ourselves, desire to be free from oppression. The English agents among us take alarm or pretend to take alarm, and they summon into the town all the military forces at their command. You see the town full of armed police, but we advise you not to offend by word or act any of these police. They are Irishmen like ourselves, feeling the same yoke perhaps, many of them our friends possibly, but appearing from circumstances to be our enemies.

"[Signed]          By order of the committee."

That handbill was gall and wormwood to the two stipendiary special magistrates and the half a dozen other magistrates who came to town to suppress the meeting. They tried many of their arts during the evening to get the promoters of it to withdraw. Friendly policemen and the wives of friendly policemen who lived in town went secretly to the wives and mothers of some of our men and in the greatest confidence whispered to them that there would be bloody work that night, as the police had got secret instructions to be ready to fire on the mob. About six o'clock in the evening a very worthy woman sent for me. She was the mother of one of our most active young men. I went to gee her. She told me she had it in the greatest confidence and on the very best authority that the police were going to shoot us down that night, and she begged of me to stop the demonstration. I told her it was not in my power to do such a thing; that I was only one of a committee of twenty, and that what she heard wes only one of the tricks of the police magistrates to frighten the people. She entreated me to listen seriously to her, and at last she went down on her knees begging of me to grant her request and save the people. It was about as painful a position as ever I was in, because she was a woman worthy of respect and every one of her family was dear to me. I had to tear myself away from her and go to discharge the duty laid upon me as being one of the committee to load the procession. As I got to the head of the procession at the place of meeting the torches were lighting, and the husband of the lady I had just left was trying to extinguish them as fast as they were lit. He too had been persuaded that we were about to be shot down if we went on with the procession, and he was using physical force to save us from destruction. He was a man all the town respected, and none of the boys would hurt him. They relit the torches as fast as tie extinguished them. The time came for the procession to move, the committee were at the bead of it, and as the orders were given to march, the magistrates, surrounded by police, blocked the street in front and stopped our march. O'Connell, the big stipendiary magistrate, was in command of the forces. He spoke and ordered us to disperse. We made a move to advance and were stopped by the police. "Who are the ringleaders of this business?" asked O'Connell of the commander of the police? "Here they are, your honor," said the policeman, pointing out the men of the committee, Conn O'Callaghan, Daniel McCartie, Morty Moynahan, John O'Driscoll, Tom O'Sullivan, Patrick Carey, Charles McCarthy, Jerry O'Meara, O'Donovan Rossa and some others. O'Connell, addressing us, said: "I order this meeting to disperse."   I

Committee—Why don't you let it disperse? You and the police are blocking the way here.

"This is a riotous and illegal meeting and I cannot allow it to go on."

"‘Tis a quiet and orderly meeting and a quite legal one, and it is you and your police who are making the riotous demonstration."

O'Connell—I'll read the Riot act, and then if you don't disperse you will have to take the consequences.

Committee—You had better do nothing that will bring on a fight here. We are not to be intimidated, and if you order bloodshed let the consequences be on your head.

We were some 4,000 strong and the police about 400. Ho read the Riot act and again ordered the people to disperse, as the meeting was illegal.

Committee—What do you see illegal in the meeting?

"Those flags there."

"Take down those flags and banners along the line of procession!"

The men passed the command along, and in a minute, from front to roar of the procession—a quarter of a mile long--every flag was lowered.

"Now do you see anything else in the procession that you would call illegal?""Those torches there."

"Put out those torches!" and the torches were extinguished immediately,

"Anything else illegal, Mr. O'Connell?"

"I order you to disperse."

"Then let us disperse; you and those police there are blocking the way on us, and we cannot move. Get out of our way."

He and the other magistrates moved aside and ordered the police to move aside. We passed on, and in regular order the procession marched through every street of the town before it dispersed. It came to a halt at the Windmill Hill; there a few of the torches were lit, and an address of sympathy with the Poles was read.

One of the flags in the procession was a red flag, with a triangle on it, and this John O'Gorman—now in Tuckahoe, N.Y.—Jerri O'Meara and John O'Driscoll, took to the top of my house, and planted on the chimney. Next day the flag caused as great a flutter among the authorities as the procession did. The people coming into town saw the red flag floating, and the rumor spread that the long wished for fight with the enemy was surely coining at last. O'Connell sent for me, and asked me to take down the flag. I told him it wasn't illegal to have it up; it was not a shamrock flag or a sunburst flag or a harp of Erin flag, it was only a simple piece of red calico, and certainly that wasn't going to frighten the British empire. I wasn't going to take it down. He threatened he would have me brought up for sentence, and I told him he may do his best. I was not called up that time, but the flag remained up till the breezes tore it down.

I soon came to learn that the landlords in my neighborhood had resolved to boycott me, The landlords' agents had whispered it into the ears of the tenants that the masters did not at all approve of buying goods at my store. Tom Somerville, the deputy Lord Lieutenant of the county used to buy farm seeds in my store; he wrote to me asking would I not look before me and see how unbecoming it was in me to be pursuing a course that was likely to ruin my business, ruin myself and ruin my family. Somerville was a great friend of mine—that is, friend in the way of doing many little things which a man in power is often in the way of doing for a man in trade. One branch of my business was the wine and spirit business; it was necessary to get my license renewed every year, and every year since I was put to prison some policeman came forward at Petty Sessions to object to my getting a license. The objection held good, the Petty Sessions magistrates would not grant the license, I'd have to appeal to the barrister at Quarter Sessions, Tom Somerville always sat alongside the barrister and whispered my license into his ear. How Tom Somerville became a great friend of mine is a little story in itself, and as it will make a little bit of light reading in the progress of Fenianism I'll tell it:

When I was young I had the making of a poet in me, and had I cultivated poetry instead of politics during my life I could easily be richer than I am today. Politics do not pay well when they are run by straight people. It is only when you run them crooked that you grow rich. It was by rhyming I came to earn Tom Somerville's friendship. He had a son in the Crimean war. Thu account came one day that he was killed, and the father was in great grief. Many of the neighbors wore sorry, because Tom Somerville was a very easy going, plausible man, and as fine a looking man as you'd see in a day's walk. A few days after the account of the son's death came another account came that he was living and then came the news that he was coming home, and there was rejoicing among his friends. I had the reputation of being the local poet and I was asked to write some lines for the occasion. I wrote them. I sent them to Tom Somerville, and next day, when he came to town, he called at my house and thanked me, telling me to look upon him as a friend while he lived. The lines are still In my head and I'll get them out now. Here they are:

Martial child of Erin's isle and brilliant son of Mars,

All hail and welcome thou to home from the Crimean wars

Joined in one mass are creed and class, their mead of praise to shower

On thee, as one of Ireland's sons, who scaled the Russian's tower.

Regarding nought but honor, no coward thought appalled you,

To make you shun the battle when a soldier's duty called you.

How oft we read the fatal lists, with joy our hearts oft bounded,

On finding that your name was not among the killed or wounded.

Much pleasure beamed on every face and gladly felt the muse,

As a respected father's life depended on the news;

So fondly has he loved that child—a true and only son

So fatal might the tidings prove, to read that he was gone.

Oh, then, how picture his despair? Thank God 'Ha different far,

Much peace and joy await him now, as ended is the War.

Escaped from death and danger, a valiant son of Erin,

Returning to his native home from battling 'gainst the alien.

Valor's representative, Skibbereen will gladly greet him

Imbued with feelings of respect she joyfully will meet him.

Loudly to home she'll welcome him, old friends, old scenes—say rather

Like one arisen from the dead—around him she will gather,

Enjoyed to see that he again has met his honored father.

That father died two years ago at Drisbane Castle, Caetletownsend. I was sorry to have lost a personal friend even though he was a friend in the service of the enemy. The son, about whom those acrostic lines were written—Major Thomas Somerville—succeeds the father to the magistracy and the lieutenancy of the County Cork. I suppose he would have to discharge the duty of arresting me now if I put in au appearance in my native home.

I began to feel that the boycotting of me by the landlords and the landlords' agents was bidding fair to ruin my trade. Farmers, who would want to buy farm seeds of me, would come in the middle of the night and wake me up out of bed to get supplied with what they wanted. Some landlords paid half the price of the clover seeds and hay seeds the tenants put in the ground, but if the seeds were bought from me they'd pay nothing. As a matter of course, those customers of mine had to buy elsewhere. In the peaceful, constitutional agitation of Irishmen for Ireland's rights England can get tile better of Ireland any day. With all the arts of man you cannot get the bettor of England unless you have power to strike her down. If you organize to get that power and she sees you have it, then she is likely to make concession. My experience of England—and I have a pretty fair experience of her, having been a prisoner in ten of her English prisons—runs parallel with James Anthony Froude's experience of her. Froude, in his lectures in New York, tried to teach our Irish people a lesson, but our Irish People will not learn it. He told them if they wanted Ireland's freedom they would have to pay for it the price of freedom—they would have to pay blood for it, because it is by blood England won it. Irish patriots in America and Irish patriots in Ireland would win Ireland's freedom by oratory and tall talk and by slashing newspaper editorials. Froude tells us "it is not to oratory and newspaper articles that the fates award the crown of national independence." But when the fates award the crown of patriotism to the orators and the newspaper writers who do the tall talking and writing, it is little wonder that Fenianism has temporarily collapsed, and that the oleomargarine article of patriotism has temporarily taken its place.

Froude's words in New York thirteen years ago made an impression on my mind; they fell waste upon the minds of others. It is worth while to record them here; it will be necessary to bear them in mind in my future letters, because I am now leaving Ireland for a while and coming to America, where abound in thousands the patriots who free Ireland without striking a blow—the patriots who prey upon the miseries and misfortunes of a fallen land and a stricken people.

"I am not here in New York," said Mr. Froude, "to talk commonplaces about English tyranny or Irish acrimony, but the fact remains that at this day, after seven hundred years of forced connection, we are still unmatched. If the votes of the Irish population were taken man for man two-thirds would ask for a separation, immediate and eternal. It stands confessed before all the world that after all our efforts we have not made friends. Seven centuries of injury divide us. They desire us simply to get away and leave them to manage their own affairs, in their own way, as best they please.  •  •  • To have to part from a high spirited and brilliant race, to whom we have given our laws and language, who have distinguished themselves in our institutions, would be a disgrace to our statesmanship, but if the Irish persisted in it we could not deny that the experiment of a forced union has been tried long enough. We should be obliged then to bid them God speed.

“But philosophers have not yet discovered how to uproot the soil of Ireland, and so long as England remains a great power, with fleets and navies and commercial interests in every corner of the world, England cannot, England will not let go her hold upon an island lying close under her side. She cannot risk the possibility of a hostile State establishing itself between her and the Atlantic. She will not consent to separation, or to measures designed to bring it about. We cannot commit political suicide. Until England is beaten to her knees Ireland must share the fortunes of the stronger country. If the Irish race refuses to be reconciled to us then we must continue as we are—each a thorn in the other's side—or they must themselves seek another home, or else they must fight for their independence and win it like men.

“Should they achieve such an enterprise, though my duty would then be to my country, and though I would struggle to hold Ireland to its obedience, yet as a member of the great brotherhood, when it was done I should willingly welcome them as another among the nations of the earth.

"But political freedom, gentlemen, is too precious a jewel to be lightly owned. It is not to oratory and newspaper articles that the fates award the crown of national independence. That crown is the reward only of united, persistent determination to be free—a determination which flinches from no danger, admits of no compromise, but expresses itself in deed as well as in word.  •  •  •  To fasten independence upon a people who have never earned it is to give wings to those who have never learned to fly. If I were to sum up in one sentence the secret of Ireland's misfortune, I should say it lay in this: That while from the first she has resisted England, complained of England, appealed to Heaven and earth against the wrongs England has inflicted on her, she has ever invited others to help her, and never herself made an effectual fight with her own forces, •  •  • Until Ireland is united in its determination to have liberty or die, independence would be a curse to her.   •   •   •  Separation we cannot agree to."

Froude speaks what England feels on the question of Ireland's independence. It is nonsense, and waste of time and money, and hope and expectation, for Irishmen to be thinking or trying to get freedom for Ireland any other way but the way Froude points out "They must fight for it like men; they must strike England to her knees."
