VIII

THE FENIAN MOVEMENT

An Account of its Origin, Progress

and Temporary Collapse.

By Jeremiah O'Donovan Rossa

 

The Fenian Fair in Chicago—The Rev. David Bell—Henry O'Clarence McCarthy—Visit to Manchester, Liverpool, &e.—Suddenly Called Back to Dublin—The Rotunda Meeting—The Agitators Skedaddle—The Moral Force Men's Second Meeting—Assaulted—No Help for it. James Stephens Visits America—Appoints Charles J. Kickham, John O'Leary, Thomas C. Luby Executive—James Stephens' Return. Meeting His Centers at Delgany—Next Year (1861) the Year of Action-1 Visit Ulster. Meet Orangemen, Ribbonmen, Presbyterians, Catholics and Protestants—Prison Rhymes, Saxon and Gaelic—Afraid of the Priest.

 

The Fenians of America had made up their minds It would be well to hold a grand Irish fair in Chicago, hi the Spring of MIL and requisition had been made upon the organization in Ireland to send on anything that could be collected in tile shape of old Irish relies, books, pictures, etc. Henry O'Clarence McCarthy came to Ireland as a commissioner from the projectors of the fair, and also as a commissioner from John O'Mahony to examine and report upon the condition of the Irish Revolutionary Organization in Ireland, England and Scotland. The Irish People office was made the depot for the shipping of any articles collected for the fair, and every day there came to us parcels of rare books, pictures and swords, guns, croppy pikes And other articles of old Ireland's manufactures and produce. These were carefully packed and taken to the United States by Mr. McCarthy.

During the time Mr. McCarthy was in Ireland he did his best to acquire a knowledge of the strength of the organization. To give him assistance in that matter I was instructed to visit Liverpool, Manchester and other parts of England with him. It was on this tour I met for the first time the Rev. David Bell (now in New York), at a hotel in Manchester. I was not personally acquainted with Bell before, but I knew him well by reputation. He was a prominent actor in the Tenant League agitation of 1852 and '53, he was then an Episcopal or Presbyterian clergyman in the north of Ireland. O'Reilly, the hotel keeper in Manchester, introduced me to him; we got talking, and before the talking ended he signified his willingness to join heart and hand in the movement we were organizing to make Ireland free and independent of England. Here in Manchester I will leave him for the present, but he will come my way by and by again.  

When Henry O'Clarence McCarthy and myself were in Liverpool, George Archdeacon, who died in New York some years ago, came to our hotel one morning, bringing me a telegram from James Stephens asking me to be in Dublin that night. There was just time to catch the train which would enable me to make Dublin in the evening, and off for Dublin Mr. McCarthy and myself started. We reached there about 7 o'clock. I saw James Stephens. He told me the business I was wanted for was to attend a public meeting that was to be held at the Rotunda at 8 o'clock that evening. I was told the part I had to take at that meeting. He asked me did I think I would be man enough for it? I told him I would try — would do my best, anyway. He smiled, gave me a few platform tickets and we parted. I went to a barber's shop, got shaved, changed my traveling clothes, got to the Rotunda platform in good time, looking some one and twenty years younger than I look to-day. Looking at the Irishmen who were at that Rotunda meeting that night, and looking at them to-day, it makes me feel as if I was going through a graveyard reading on the tombstones the names of a generation of brave men past and gone. Icily cold falls on my ears the cry of the sham patriots of Ireland who tell us to “bide our time” and “wait for the opportunity,” when I see dying out, year after year, the men who in early life would shed their hearts' blood for the cause, or for any good cause at all.

This meeting at the Rotunda that I am now to speak of came about this way: The peaceable, constitutional and parliamentary agitators of that time were just the same as the peaceable, constitutional and parliamentary agitators of the present time; they were as good men and their principles were as good. All the Irishmen of that time respected John Martin, the '48 rebel, as much as all the Irishmen of the present day respect Charles Stewart Parnell. But Irishmen of my class respect principles more than men. John Martin got in among the agitators twenty-one years ago, and he with them had to fly from that Rotunda meeting before the meeting was ended.

The English flunkey element of Dublin were going to erect a monument in College Green to Queen Victoria's husband, who had died a short time before that, and the Irish Parliamentary party wrote and spoke protestingly against such a thing being done. The Dublin corporation voted a site in College Green for the statue, and the parliamentary patriots called an indignation meeting to scold the whole scheme out of existence. It may seem strange that the Fenians should set their face against such a meeting as that, but they did it, and the reason of their doing it was this: They did not want to see the moral force agitators acquire the popularity of patriots, by protesting against the erection of a Prince Albert statue in College Green—which popularity they would surely use to crush the revolutionary movement that would hurl into the Liffey or into the sea every monument of English domination that raised its head in the land. James Stephens called around him the centers of Dublin and stated the case to them. He considered it wise there and then to put a stop to the game the agitating patriots were playing. The centers concurred in his wisdom and decided to marshal their men at the Rotunda on the night of the meeting.

Alexander M. Sullivan, who died last year, was editor of the Nation newspaper. He was the active man in getting up the meeting. He was most obnoxious to the Fenians on account of his actions regarding them in the past and him particularly it was decided to pay off at that mooting. I had come into newspaper controversy with him on a few occasions, and it was considered well to have me at that Rotunda meeting to confront him. That is why I was telegraphed for to England. There was to be no unfriendly interference with the meeting if Mr. Sullivan was not made a prominent figure in it; but if he was made a prominent figure I was to propose that he be expelled that meeting, and the men in the body of the hall were to make way for him to pass out. If he did not go quietly through the channel that was made for him, a few men were to take hold of him and escort him out.

The chair was taken and the speechmaking commenced. The O’Donoghue of the Glens — the Parnell of that day —commenced his speech. He spoke standing upon a large table that was on the platform; he alluded to his "esteemed friend, A. M. Sullivan," and no sooner were the words out of his mouth than the uproar commenced. The chairman called for order, but no order would come. I wished to have O'Donoghue heard, and I went upon the table alongside of the O'Donoghue and asked the people to listen to him; the table would not stand It; the legs of it gave way; there was a crush; I fell into the arms of the people who were in front of the platform. I don't know how the O'Donoghue fell. I was put back upon the stage, and when I looked around I saw the whole crowd of the O'Donoghue people and Sullivan people skedaddling through the back doors. In five minutes' time from the crash of the table there was not a man on that platform but Fenians, and there they were going on with all kinds of fun. One man held aloft on a cross leg of the table the green baize cloth that had covered the table, singing "Up for the Green;" another held aloft another cross leg, proclaiming himself a missoner who was leading a crusade against the infidels. The Dublin papers next morning said it was the most good humored mob Dublin over witnessed, but that its organization was dangerous to the government, as it bespoke discipline and determination to be successful in its purpose.

The moral force patriots were very much annoyed that their meeting was broken up by tile Fenians; they announced they would hold another meeting that day week; that they would hold it In the middle of the tiny instead of at night, and that admission was to be had only by tickets, which tickets were to be got at the Nation office. A volunteer guard was called for who were to have charge of the doors and be prepared to repel the advance into the building of any Fenian suspects. The day of meeting came, and I had plenty of tickets of admission. I got seine from Dr. Edward Power, who was subsequently a convict with me, and who died in Oxford, N. J., two years ago. I got more from Eugene Nolan, of Carlow, who was a medical student at the Catholic university, a brave dashing Irishman, dead also. In fact our friends had as many tickets as they desired, because in Dublin that time there was hardly an Irishman of any spirit who was not a member or a sympathizer of our organization.

I presented myself at one of the doors of the Rotunda the day of the meeting. I showed my ticket to one usher; it was good and he passed me in; but the usher inside of him recognized me and cried: "Hold!" I was recognized and was told I could not pass in. I remonstrated, but it was no use. I asked to have my ticket back, but could not get it; police were around me, and I knew they were there at the requisition of the projectors of the meeting, and would have me taken to the lockup If I gave the least excuse for arrest. One of the police told me it was better for me go away, and as he said that, one of the ushers named Joseph Hanley caught me by the beard and shoved me back. "Ah, you're a mean coward," was all I could say or do; the police had me shoved aside in a moment. I don't let myself got vexed often, but I was vexed that day —vexed to think that fellow should, under the protection of the police, catch me as he did; but there was no help for it unless I wanted to got myself taken to jail, and then my friends even would say I lost my head and lost my wits in the battle. I learned from one of our men that James Stephens was in a house convenient watching the proceedings. I went to the house and met him. "Captain," said I, "you see those lower windows of the Rotunda, we can scale those windows and go in; what do you say?" "I order you to go right straight to the office in Parliament street and stay there till I meet you." I had to obey orders. I went to the office; the captain and others turned up by and by and talked and laughed over the adventures of the day. The most ridiculous feature in the attitude of the agitators was that here were they meeting to protest against the erection of a monument to Queen Victoria's husband in Ireland and they wore obliged to apply to Queen Victoria's deputy in Ireland to protect them while holding the meeting. They had made special application for an extra police force at the Rotunda that second day of meeting and the extra force was there. But it was evident the agitators were crushed and the backbone of moral force agitation was broken in Ireland. Those meetings at the Rotunda showed the strength of the organization, and also showed the agents of England in Ireland that they had to be on their guard. The English know that Ireland has always in it the food for rebellion, and they use every art of church and State to destroy it. To send the Irish out of Ireland has always been England's grand panacea for the pacification of Ireland. It is her panacea to-day; it was her panacea last year when she was paying the passage from Ireland to America of even the unarmed or armless people whom the operation of her Landlord laws had driven into the poorhouses of Ireland. "To hell or to Connaught with them" was the cry in days of old; to-day she does not consider it safe to have them even in Connaught, and as she cannot send them direct to hell she must ship them to America, if America will let her.

The goods that were collected in Dublin for the Chicago fair had all been shipped to America; James Stephens considered it would promote the interests of the organization to have him take a trip across the Atlantic, and the trip was made. He had commissioned his work in Ireland to an executive body of three, consisting of Mr. Luby, Mr. O'Leary and Charles J. Kickham. This commission was given In writing and the written document turned up at the trials afterward, having been found at the house of Mr. Luby the night of his arrest; it was known as the executive document.

James Stephens remained in America during the summer of 1864. He visited several camps of the Union Army, visited several cities, was at the Chicago fair and brought with him back to Ireland most of the money proceeds of that fair. I think it was about August he returned to Ireland. He set vigorously to work to strengthen the organization, to encourage the men and to give thorn hope of immediate action. He appointed a day for meeting the centers who were in the districts in the neighborhood of Dublin, and about forty or fifty of them attended. They came from the counties of Wicklow, Wexford, Carlow, Kildare, Kilkenny, Queens, Kings, Tipperary, Galway, Westmeath, Meath, Louth and Dublin. The meeting was on a Sunday, at a hotel in Delgany, in the County Wicklow. Dinner had been previously engaged for the party, as if they were an excursion party from Dublin. After dinner the room was cleared of all but the invited guests, and the Captain, as we used to call Mr. Stephens, stood up to tell us the news. He gave cheering accounts of all he saw in America and told us, collectively and individually, to bear in mind that next year was to be the year of action—the year of Ireland's fight for Ireland's freedom. When ho said that, there were cheers upon cheers in that dining hall and every center who was present went back to his district rejoicing that he had such glorious news to tell his men.

When I met Mr. Stephens next day he told me the north of Ireland and the west of Ireland should be visited and be informed of the good news he brought from America. He had decided to send me to the north and to make connection with John Nolan, of Carlow, who was doing business in Belfast and who would give me introductions to the most important men. I was then to travel through the district of Connaught and to make connections with Edward Duffy, of Roscommon, who would travel around with me. Mr. Stephens had sent other organizers through Leinster and Munster, and very soon the men in every county of Ireland were thinking of nothing but of the coming fight next year. The pike makers who were in Dublin were instructed to carry on their work vigorously, the number of organizers among the English soldiers was increased and several of the centers in England were asked over to Dublin to hold consultation with the chief of the organization. Before I left Dublin on my mission for Ulster I happened to say something to the captain that went very near making him eat the face off me. While he was in America the task of running the newspaper fell upon my shoulders, not the task of editing it or writing for it, because in that department were Messrs. O'Leary, Luby, Kickham and Mulcahy, but the task of finding money to pay the printers and pay all the other demands that were connected with the business department. I found It pretty hard to make ends meet sometimes, as the priests and the landlords in several places were breaking up our agencies and the money returns were consequently decreasing, so I suggested to Mr. Stephens that it would be well to hold some of his resources in reserve for the paper, lest the promised monthly supplies from America should fail to arrive. He got vexed at my suggesting this; he said the supplies should come and that he had made his calculations and had based the success of his work on the fulfillment of the promises made to him. He would spend in preparation and in organization for the coming month what he had to spend, even though a dollar of the supplies promised for the month after that were never to come. In one sense he was right, but in another sense I thought it would be no harm to hold a little of the fund in reserve for fear of accident. That is one thing I have to say of James Stephens: he never hoarded up any of the organization money in view of a rainy day coming in the future of his own life, or in the future of any life connected with him, When he got money from America he promptly spent it in furthering the work of the organization. I provided myself with letters of introduction to several good men in Ulster who had been in the Tenant League movement, but who had been dead in politics since the Sadliers and Keoghs put a nail in its coffin. I had a. letter to Dr. McKnight, of Derry, who was editor of the Derry Journal, and to Thomas Neilson Underwood, of Strabane, who was a grandnephew of Thomas Neilson, the United Irishman of 1798. I met these gentlemen, and from them I got letters to others. I thus got into the Presbyterian element of the people.

I met John Nolan in Belfast; he had charge of the carpet department of a large dry goods house on the square; he could not well got away from business till Saturday evening and we made arrangements to go to Newtownards in the County Down on Sunday morning. Sunday morning came and we found ourselves in Newtownards. In the course of the day we found ourselves at the hotel In company with thirty or forty men, who had already been sworn in its members of the organization. The impression that meeting made on my mind remains on it still and will remain on it so long as I remain on earth. About a third of the men were Catholics, a third Presbyterians and a third Episcopalian Protestants. A few years before that the Protestants had been Orangemen and the Catholics had been Ribbonmen, having no association or acquaintanceship with each other, but one hating the other as if it were a necessary article of his religion. The Orangeman admitted that before he joined the society he imagined he had no enemy on earth but the Ribbonman, and the Ribbonman admitted that before he joined he imagined he had no greater enemy in the world than the Orangeman; each acknowledged that he considered he could do no better act on earth than to slay the other; and there they were that day around that board shaking hands and thanking God that their eyes were opened and that they were meeting as brothers in the common cause of fatherland.

Present at that meeting was Colonel William MacWilliams, a Presbyterian resident of Newtownards, who a few years ago lived in Baltimore and was on the staff of the Governor of Maryland, and who is now living in Palatka, Fla. The memory of that day spent in the "black north" brightened many of the darkest days of my prison life afterward, and I cherish the recollection of it still. Immerged in a black hole cell in the English prison of Chatham, with no light and with little food, I found exercise for my thoughts in reflections upon the sad condition of affairs in my native land that set brother fighting against brother—to the destruction of their liberty and their happiness, and I passed away the hours stretched in that dark dungeon composing verses that spoke the thoughts passing through my mind. If there be any poetry in the verses it was Newtownards inspired it. There is strong language in them no doubt—language stronger than I dare speak to-day, because to-day I am surrounded by the busy world outside a prison, amenable to all the world's prejudices and punishable for trespassing upon them. But just as a matter of curiosity and to show how free the soul of a man can be even when his body is chained, I will print hero the prison rhymes I am speaking of:

 

IRELAND OVER THE WATER.

Here's a health to the victims of tyranny's wrong:

Here's a health to the weak who're oppressed by the strong;

Hero's a health to the men, be their creed what it may,

Who can say "God Savo Ireland" wherever they pray.

And let them kneel to God above,

In church or chapel, kirk or grove,

Here's heart and hand, and hope and love,

For Ireland, over the water.

Our long suffering mother is ravished by knaves,

And, dishonored, she weepingly nurses us slaves.

The tyrants have made us a hell upon earth,

And we labor in chains, while they revel in mirth.

We'd hardly be more sorely tried,

More scattered through the world wide,

Had Christ by us been crucified,

In Ireland, over the water.

But we're told our misfortunes are owing to our guilt;

That we're paying for the blood which our forefathers spilt;

That England to us is the heaven-sent stroke,

And we strike against God when we strike at her yoke.

This teaching finds us blood red graves.

In lands beyond the salt sea waves,

And leaves us crouching, cringing slaves,

In Ireland, over the water.

It is said, and I think 'tis by Machiavel,

That tyrants in teaching the Bible excel,

In order the better to plunder the poor

And make them submit to tile wrongs they endure.

And thus they offer us "the Word"--

They tell us pray and trust the Lord

And then they rob with tire and sword,

In Ireland, over the water.

To our ruthless invaders the creed mattered naught,

They made heaven subserve every conquest they sought,

And the Catholics sent by the Popes for our pence

Just hit us as hard as the Puritan saints.

Since Adrian's grant, 'Us fraud, 'tie force,

'Tis Bulls, 'tis bayonets, foot and horse,

'Tis Cullen's or 'tis Cromwell's curse,

In Ireland, over the water.

Can the creeds that love freedom and manhood elsewhere

Be fruitful of nothing but slavery there?

Will the "Protestant Boys" never give us a hope,

But, hugging their fetters, afraid of a Pope?

Our Pagan sires our strifes would shun;

They saw their heaven through the sun;

Their God smiled down on every one

In Ireland, over the water.

Our children of Roman and Protestant birth

Proclaim our disgrace through the brothels of earth.

Yet the preachers preach on—we have nothing to do

But to "carry the Cross" and "give Caesar his due."

Christ never said 'twas Caesar's coin

The land is ours. Then let us join

Our hearts and hands across the Boyne,

For Ireland, over the water.

But the bigots start up to prevent the embrace,

And the phantoms of faction are flung in our face.

"To hell with the Pope"—"hell with William the Third"

Thou like devils we fight "for the love of the Lord."

The world's contempt rewards our pains—

We're slaves, and with our very chains

We batter out each other's brains,

In Ireland, over the water.

This creed of dissension is nursed in the land,

While the creed of our martyrs is prisoned and banned.

Sheares, Crowley, Fitzgerald, Lynch, Duffy and Tone,

Emmet, Larkin and Orr died for Ireland alone.

For freedom's cause at freedom's shrine—

This was the creed of Mike O'Brien

Let it be yours as well as mine,

For Ireland over the water.

And then for a struggle to end in success

When the Keoghs will protect us, and Cullens will bless;

For ‘tis but for failure that "rebels" are damned,

That scaffolds are mounted and prisons are crammed

Come North and South our land to save

Can't we be Irish, true and bravo,

And neither Rome's nor England's slave

In Ireland, over the water.

I further occupied my time by translating those lines into the Gaelic tongue: there are Highlanders and Inlanders reading the BROOKLYN EAGLE who read Gaelic, and apart from the history of the Fenian movement they will not be displeased that this paper will take to their homes the following Gaelic translation of the foregoing verses:

EIRE TRASNA 'N T-SAILE. 

[Gaelic Verses repeat the poem above and are omitted from this transcription]

 

When I look over those lines and over the sentiments they speak, I begin to think how much more free and independent the Soul of a man is when he is in prison than when he is out of prison. The thought would never come into my head to-day of recording such sentiments in prose or verse, though I live in free America to-day. And it is very likely that even for reproducing them In the EAGLE I will subject myself to the censure of very many narrow minds—well, not narrow minds, but selfish minds—minds that will not express themselves as they feel and think, because doing so is dangerous to pocket and popularity. That is another of the causes of the collapse of Fenianism. Its temporary failure led to its unpopularity, its unpopularity led to the creation of the opinion that it was not respectable to be associated with it; the friends of England cast ridicule upon it, and the friends of Ireland allowed them, and thus allowed them to create the giant which now crushes them, the giant that makes a man like me a boycotted man in society, an outcast in society I may say, because I dare to be free and independent enough to speak as I think. Nine-tenths of the Irishmen in America feel and think about Ireland and England as I do, feel and think that Ireland or any other enslaved country is justified in using toward its emancipation such arms and means of warfare as the enslaver is using and is ready to use toward its enslavement. If I speak individually to any Irishman in New York or Brooklyn to-day, nine out of ten of the Irish born people agree with me in every opinion I hold. If I speak to them collectively, or rather if they express themselves collectively at public meeting, nine out of ten of them will publicly condemn the vary things they privately or personally approve. This is slavery, and it helps very much to hold Ireland enslaved, to see Ireland's children in America afraid to speak the truth, thus allowing the opinion to grow that the means England uses to carry on her wars and to hold subject nations enslaved are honorable, while if Ireland or the subject nations use the same means to rid themselves of the enslavement they are dishonorable.           

The week after the Sunday I spent at Nowtownards I went to Newry to Scarva, to Guilford to Loughbrickland, to Rathfriland to Carrickfergus, to Lisburn, Antrim, Randalstown, Ballycastle, Ballymoney and Ballymena. John Nolan, now dead and buried in America, was with me in some of the places, and some very strange experiences came our way. A friend in Ballymena was seeing us off on the train that was going to start for Randalstown. We were waiting at the Ballymena Station till the train would be ready to start, when suddenly our companion darted away from us, and ran behind a railway carriage. We were surprised; we got into a carriage; he came to the window at the off side of the station, and asking him what was the matter. "Didn't you see the priest coming up?" said he, "he knows me, and he'd know ye were strangers, and if he saw me in your company he would immediately suspect what your business was." This man was not afraid of the police or of the magistrates, to be seen with us, but when he saw Father Kelly, his parish priest coining toward us, he ran away and hid himself.
